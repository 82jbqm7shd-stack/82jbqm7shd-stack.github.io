Mark Hopking

Mark Richard Hopking (born 28 April 1971) is a British mariner and superyacht captain. His career includes expedition sailing and senior command appointments in the superyacht sector, including command of the environmental expedition yacht 2041 and later the 78-metre motor yacht Hampshire II.[1][12]

Overview

Hopking’s maritime career began in the early 1990s in Scotland. He served as crew aboard the yacht English Rose VI, a vessel associated with the 1977–78 Whitbread Round the World Race under the name Debenhams.[2][3][4]

During the 1990s he was involved in an expedition associated with sea kayaking around Cape Horn, documented in contemporaneous press and later media sources, including a 1993 ITV film and an episode of Channel 4’s Cutting Edge documentary strand.[5][7][9]

In 1999 he became captain of the expedition yacht 2041, acquired for an Antarctic Treaty awareness programme; the programme and voyage are described in published sources by Robert Swan and collaborators.[11][12]

After moving into the superyacht sector in the 2000s, Hopking later commanded the 78-metre motor yacht Hampshire II and subsequently served as project manager and build captain on the 74-metre Feadship explorer yacht Sherpa, a build covered by industry publications and documentary material.[14][16][17]

Maritime career

Hopking’s early maritime experience is associated with the John Ridgway Adventure School in Sutherland, Scotland.[2] He later worked with The Challenge Business during the period when it organised the Global Challenge ocean races; a 1998 profile in The Daily Telegraph discussed his career and personal financial planning in that context.[10]

His later career includes command roles in the superyacht sector and involvement in new-build and commissioning activity. Industry coverage of his contributions includes publication in The Crew Report and reporting in specialist yachting media.[14][15]

Further detail (vessels, dates, and project roles) is presented on marine.markhopking.co.uk with full citations and a conservative approach to privately-held documentation.

Barony of Balvill

Hopking holds the Scottish feudal barony of Balvill.[19] The dignity was created by Crown charter in 1630 in favour of Archibald Campbell, Lord Lorne (later 8th Earl and 1st Marquess of Argyll), and forms part of the historic Scottish system of feudal baronies; subsequent legal developments include the Abolition of Feudal Tenure etc. (Scotland) Act 2000.[19]

A detailed historical narrative, source list, and terminology (including styles of address) are presented on barony.markhopking.co.uk. Where certain evidence is held privately (e.g., certificates), it is described as such and not presented as publicly verifiable.

Heraldry and tartan

Hopking was granted arms by the Court of the Lord Lyon in 2018, recorded in the Public Register of All Arms and Bearings in Scotland.[20][21]

He also holds a personal tartan recorded in the Scottish Register of Tartans.[22]

The heraldic achievement and related materials (including the tartan entry) are presented on heraldry.markhopking.co.uk.

Personal life

Hopking lives in Edinburgh, Scotland. He has two children.

He is a Fellow of the Society of Antiquaries of Scotland and maintains an interest in Scottish heraldry and genealogy.[23]
